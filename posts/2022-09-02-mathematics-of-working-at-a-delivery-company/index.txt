8:T7b76,<h1>Mathematics of working at a delivery company</h1>
<p>Last year my friend worked at a delivery company. Their job was to deliver groceries to customers on a bicycle. In their company, the wage was based on the amount of orders per hour they delivered. For example:</p>
<ul>
<li>X euros for 4 orders per hour</li>
<li>Y euros for 6 orders per hour</li>
<li>Z euros for 8 orders per hour</li>
<li>Î© euros for 10 orders per hour</li>
</ul>
<p>But, &quot;What is the wage of a delivery driver?&quot;, I thought. Sure, it cannot be consistent, but on average it should be similar.</p>
<p><em><strong>TL;DR</strong>: most-likely the wage rate won't be above the minimal (X euros for 4 orders per hour).</em></p>
<p>First let's formulate the problem in a mathematical way.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Monkeys is an imaginary grocery delivery service. Their warehouse is located in the center of a city with radius <span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML"><semantics><mrow><mi>R</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">R</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height:0.6833em;"></span><span class="mord mathnormal" style="margin-right:0.00773em;">R</span></span></span></span>. The clients are uniformly distributed in the circle. What is the expected value of delivery time?</p>
</blockquote>
<p>So, the delivery man starts off in the center (warehouse), brings the groceries to the customer and comes back to get ready for the next delivery.</p>
<p><img src="./assets/problem-setting.png" alt="problem setting"></p>
<p>The expected value of delivery time is the expected value of the distance to the customer divided by the velocity of the delivery man.</p>
